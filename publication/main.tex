\section{Methods}

\subsection{Game Environment}

We construct a heads-up no-limit Texas Hold'em \textit{turn-river
subgame}~\citep{osborne1994course}.
Each episode begins with two players, a 52-card deck, two private
hole cards per player, four public board cards already revealed,
a starting pot of \num{200} chips, and effective stacks of
\num{200} chips each.
Street progression follows standard Hold'em rules: turn action,
river card reveal (if the hand is not terminal), river action, and
showdown if neither player has folded.

\subsubsection{Action abstraction.}
When no bet is outstanding, legal actions are
$\{\texttt{check},\; \texttt{bet\_half\_pot},\; \texttt{bet\_pot},\; \texttt{jam}\}$.
When facing a bet, legal actions are
$\{\texttt{fold},\; \texttt{call},\; \texttt{jam}\}$.
This coarse abstraction is a deliberate scientific simplification that
keeps the action space tractable while preserving the qualitative
structure of bluff-value tradeoffs and pot-odds decisions.

\subsubsection{Reward structure.}
Terminal payoffs are zero-sum chip profit/loss.
Each player's scalar reward is the signed change in their stack
relative to episode start.

\subsubsection{Information structure.}
Each agent observes only its own hole cards, the public board,
pot and stack sizes, current street, betting history, and legal actions.
Opponent hole cards remain hidden until showdown.

\subsection{Hand-Class Ontology}

The central latent variable is opponent \emph{hand class}—a
board-relative category that abstracts over exact card combinations.
The default ontology comprises seven mutually exclusive,
collectively exhaustive classes:
\texttt{nuts\_or\_near\_nuts},
\texttt{strong\_made},
\texttt{medium\_made},
\texttt{weak\_showdown},
\texttt{strong\_draw},
\texttt{weak\_draw},
and \texttt{air}.
The mapping from hole cards and board to hand class is deterministic
and board-relative~\citep{chen2006mathematics}.
This ontology is configurable for sensitivity analysis.

\subsection{Agent Hierarchy}

\subsubsection{Heuristic baseline.}
The Heuristic agent applies fixed rules based on hand-strength
buckets, basic draw recognition, street-aware thresholds, and
pot-odds checks.
It never estimates expected value and maintains no model of the
opponent.
This agent serves as a transparent lower bound on performance.

\subsubsection{Static-EV agent.}
The Static-EV agent selects the action $a^*$ that maximises
\begin{equation}
  \text{EV}(a \mid s,\,\pi_0)
    = \sum_{\text{outcomes}} P(\text{outcome} \mid a,\, s,\, \pi_0)\, U(\text{outcome}),
  \label{eq:ev_static}
\end{equation}
where $s$ denotes the current public state and $\pi_0$ is a fixed
uniform prior over opponent hand classes.
Equity estimates use Monte Carlo rollout ($n = 50$ samples per
decision); opponent response probabilities are supplied by a
parameterised synthetic response model.
Crucially, this agent \emph{never updates} $\pi_0$ after observing
opponent actions.

\subsubsection{Belief-EV agent.}
The Belief-EV agent maintains a posterior $\pi_t$ over opponent hand
classes and updates it after each observed opponent action via Bayes'
theorem:
\begin{equation}
  P(c \mid a,\, s)
    \propto P(a \mid c,\, s,\, \mathcal{F})\; P(c),
  \label{eq:bayes_update}
\end{equation}
where $c$ is a hand class, $a$ is the observed action, and
$\mathcal{F}$ is the opponent family parameter.
The posterior is normalised after each update; Laplace smoothing
($\alpha = 0.05$) prevents zero-probability hand classes.
Action selection follows the same EV formula as Equation~\ref{eq:ev_static},
substituting the current posterior $\pi_t$ for the fixed prior.
Belief state is reset at the start of each hand.

\subsection{Response Model and Opponent Families}

The response model defines
$P(\text{action} \mid \text{hand class},\, \text{public state},\, \mathcal{F})$
as a parameterised synthetic function.
Opponent families are characterised by six scalar dimensions:
aggression, bluff rate, call looseness, jam propensity,
draw semibluff frequency, and trap frequency.
Table~\ref{tab:opponent_families} lists the families used in this study.
Development families (\texttt{balanced}, \texttt{aggressive},
\texttt{passive}, \texttt{maniac}, \texttt{trappy}) are used for
training-time tuning.
Held-out families (\texttt{held\_out\_1}, \texttt{held\_out\_2})
are evaluated only during robustness experiments.

\subsection{Experimental Design}

We run four pre-registered experiments.
All experiments use seeded Monte Carlo simulation for reproducibility;
identical seed and configuration always produce identical hand traces.

\subsubsection{Experiment 1 — Main head-to-head comparison.}
Each of the three agents plays as player~0 against a Heuristic
player~1 across six matchup conditions (agent $\times$ opponent
family).
Configuration: five seeds $\times$ \num{1000} hands per seed per
condition ($n = \num{5000}$ hands per cell).

\subsubsection{Experiment 2 — Belief ablation.}
Belief-EV and Static-EV play \emph{directly against each other} as
player~0 and player~1 respectively, under three opponent-family
contexts (balanced, aggressive, trappy).
This isolates the contribution of posterior updating from the
broader EV-reasoning advantage.

\subsubsection{Experiment 3 — Calibration and behavioural profiles.}
Belief-EV plays under three development opponent families
(balanced, aggressive, maniac).
Per-decision posterior entropy, action frequency profiles, and
EV margin statistics are extracted from raw hand logs.

\subsubsection{Experiment 4 — Opponent shift / robustness.}
All three agents are evaluated against the two held-out families.
Performance is compared directly to the development-set baseline
to quantify generalisation loss.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

Primary metric: mean chips won per hand (player~0 perspective).
Bootstrap confidence intervals (5,000 resamples, $\alpha = 0.05$)
are computed for all mean estimates.
Head-to-head ablation differences are tested with a paired
two-tailed $t$-test on per-hand rewards.
Effect sizes are reported as raw chip-per-hand differences.
No corrections for multiple comparisons are applied; individual
$p$-values and confidence intervals are reported in full.
All analyses are implemented in Python with NumPy, SciPy, and
pandas; source code is publicly available at
\href{https://github.com/kevinmsong/PokerBeliefStudy}{\texttt{github.com/kevinmsong/PokerBeliefStudy}}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Experiment 1: EV Reasoning Dominates Over Heuristics}

Figure~\ref{fig:main_comparison} and Table~\ref{tab:main_results}
summarise agent performance across development opponent families.
Both EV agents substantially outperform the Heuristic baseline.
Against the balanced opponent family, the Static-EV agent achieves
$174.2$ chips/hand (95\% CI: $[170.1, 178.3]$) and the Belief-EV
agent achieves $174.6$ chips/hand (95\% CI: $[170.4, 178.7]$),
compared with $97.2$ chips/hand (95\% CI: $[93.8, 100.8]$) for
the Heuristic—a gap of approximately $\mathbf{77}$ chips/hand
($\approx$\,79\%).

This ordering is consistent across all opponent families tested.
Belief-EV and Static-EV performance are nearly indistinguishable
when both face the Heuristic as an opponent:
the Belief-EV advantage is only $+0.4$ chips/hand on the balanced
opponent ($174.6$ vs.\ $174.2$), and ranges from
$-2.4$ to $+0.96$ across families.
This near-parity suggests that the benefit of posterior updating is
negligible when the opposing agent is not itself EV-rational.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Static-EV Outperforms Belief-EV in Direct Competition}

Figure~\ref{fig:belief_ablation} shows the distribution of per-hand
chip differentials (Belief-EV $-$ Static-EV) in head-to-head play.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_results} reports means, confidence intervals,
and test statistics.

The Static-EV agent wins significantly more chips/hand than the
Belief-EV agent in two of three conditions:
$-13.1$ chips/hand on the trappy family
($t(4999) = -2.36$, $p = 0.018$)
and $-11.9$ chips/hand on the aggressive family
($t(4999) = -2.12$, $p = 0.034$).
The balanced-family difference of $-9.9$ chips/hand is in the same
direction and of similar magnitude but does not reach conventional
significance at $\alpha = 0.05$
($t(4999) = -1.76$, $p = 0.078$).
Across all three conditions, the 95\% CI for the mean difference
includes zero only in the balanced case; the consistent negative
sign and effect sizes of $\approx$10--13 chips/hand represent
approximately 6--7\% of the mean pot.

\textbf{This constitutes a falsification of the central hypothesis.}
Belief updating does not improve payoff in direct competition; it
imposes a measurable performance cost.
We attribute this to response-model misspecification: the Bayesian
update in Equation~\ref{eq:bayes_update} amplifies errors in
$P(a \mid c,\, s,\, \mathcal{F})$ when the opponent is itself
EV-rational rather than drawn from the assumed family.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Posterior Entropy and Behavioural Profiles}

Figure~\ref{fig:calibration} (left) shows the distribution of
posterior entropy across opponent families.
Mean posterior entropy is nearly identical across families:
$H = 1.914$ bits (balanced), $1.914$ bits (aggressive),
and $1.913$ bits (maniac), all close to the maximum possible entropy
of $H_{\max} = \log_2 7 \approx 1.946$ bits for seven equiprobable
classes.
Standard deviations are small ($\sigma \leq 0.07$ bits).
This near-maximal entropy indicates that the posterior distribution
over opponent hand classes remains close to uniform at decision time;
observing a single opponent action provides little information for
discriminating among the seven hand classes under the parameterised
response model.

Figure~\ref{fig:calibration} (right) displays action frequency
profiles.
The Heuristic agent checks on 55.1\% of decisions and jams on only
1.2\%, reflecting its conservative rule-based logic.
The Static-EV and Belief-EV agents have nearly indistinguishable
profiles: both bet half-pot on $\approx$44--45\% of decisions and jam
on $\approx$28--29\%.
The near-identical profiles confirm that posterior updating is not
generating meaningfully different action selections compared with
the static prior under these experimental conditions.

\subsection{Experiment 4: Robustness to Held-Out Opponent Families}

Figure~\ref{fig:robustness} compares development-family performance
to performance on the two held-out families for all three agents.
Numerical results appear in Table~\ref{tab:robustness_results}.

Neither EV agent shows a meaningful performance drop on held-out
families.
Belief-EV: $+0.76$ chips/hand on held-out~1 and $+0.58$ on
held-out~2 (both negligible relative to the $\approx$150 chip
standard deviation per hand).
Static-EV: $-0.58$ on held-out~1 and $+2.21$ on held-out~2.
The Heuristic is unaffected by opponent family shift by construction
(it observes no opponent model).
Crucially, \textbf{the Belief-EV agent gains no robustness advantage
over Static-EV despite having an explicit dynamic belief model.}
Both agents transfer equivalently to novel opponent families,
consistent with the near-uniform posteriors observed in Experiment~3.

\subsection{Seed-Level Stability}

Figure~\ref{fig:seed_variance} plots per-seed mean chips/hand across
the five independent replications.
Across seeds, the Heuristic varies between $91$ and $103$
chips/hand; both EV agents vary between $163$ and $184$ chips/hand.
The \emph{rank ordering} of agents is preserved in all five seeds,
and the Belief-EV vs.\ Static-EV difference never reverses sign.
This confirms that the main findings are not artefacts of a
particular random seed.

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

Table~\ref{tab:summary} synthesises the four experiments.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \small
  \caption{%
    \textbf{Summary of all experimental findings.}
    Effect direction and magnitude relative to the reference agent.
    $\uparrow$/$\downarrow$ = outperforms/underperforms reference;
    $\approx$ = negligible difference.
  }
  \label{tab:summary}
  \begin{tabularx}{\columnwidth}{l l l l}
    \toprule
    Experiment & Comparison & Effect & Magnitude \\
    \midrule
    1 & EV vs.\ Heuristic          & $\uparrow$        & $+77$ chips/hand \\
    1 & Belief-EV vs.\ Static-EV   & $\approx$         & $+0.4$ chips/hand \\
    2 & Belief-EV vs.\ Static-EV   & $\downarrow$      & $-10$ to $-13$ chips/hand \\
      & (head-to-head)             & ($p \leq 0.034$)  & \\
    3 & Posterior entropy          & Near max          & $H \approx 1.91$ bits \\
    3 & Action profiles            & $\approx$         & $<$1\% difference \\
    4 & Robustness (EV agents)     & $\approx$         & $|\Delta| \leq 2.2$ chips/hand \\
    4 & Belief-EV robustness       & No advantage      & vs.\ Static-EV \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

The dominant result is the large, robust EV-over-heuristic effect
($+77$ chips/hand; $\approx$79\%).
The belief-updating effect is zero or negative in all conditions
where direct comparison is possible.
The near-uniform posteriors in Experiment~3 provide a mechanistic
explanation: the response model does not supply sufficient
discriminating signal to shift the posterior away from the uniform
prior, rendering belief updates uninformative and their computational
overhead a net liability.
